The Pen Has Always Won
The greatest ideas in history
started on napkins.
Amazon· 2001
The Flywheel Napkin
At the dot-com crash's lowest point, Jeff Bezos listened to Jim Collins describe the "flywheel effect" — and drew it on the nearest napkin. A heavy wheel, hard at first, that builds its own momentum.
That sketch became the blueprint for the most powerful business model in e-commerce history.
Southwest Airlines· 1967
The $5 Triangle
In a San Antonio hotel bar, Herb Kelleher and Rollin King drew a triangle on a cocktail napkin: Dallas, Houston, San Antonio. Three cities. No hubs. No layovers. A price anyone could afford.
That triangle led to 47 consecutive years of profit — the most remarkable run in aviation history.
Pixar· 1994
The Napa Valley Lunch
Four Pixar veterans ordered lunch in Napa Valley. Between courses, they scrawled ideas across napkins — rough shapes, names, worlds that didn't exist yet.
Those napkins became A Bug's Life, Monsters, Inc., Finding Nemo, and WALL·E.
Computers have existed for decades. The greatest ideas still came from pens.
